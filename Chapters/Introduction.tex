\section{Background}
\pagestyle{plain}
As enterprise data ecosystems grow in complexity, organizations increasingly rely on integrating heterogeneous data sources to support market analysis and strategic decision-making \cite{putrama2024heterogeneous}. Due to differences in data formats, classification standards, and regional business rules, companies must design complex data processing approaches to harmonize and reconcile these datasets in order to approximate real market conditions \cite{cheng2024harmonization}. To support such analytical tasks, large organizations often rely on enterprise software platforms such as SAP, which is developed by the German software company SAP SE. SAP provides integrated enterprise software products and analytical environments that support business process management, data integration, reporting, and decision support across organizational functions \cite{gessa2023erp,sap_erp_guide}. In analytical settings, SAP-based environments can be used to store, process, transform, and report large volumes of business data. While these systems provide strong capabilities for large-scale data processing, their operation is not always fully transparent to end users. When data transformations depend on system configurations, integration mechanisms, and embedded business rules, it becomes difficult to understand how outputs are generated, validate the correctness of results, and identify potential inconsistencies in the data processing pipeline \cite{acev2025datagovernance,foidl2024pipeline}. Furthermore, the separation between business users and technical system implementations introduces communication barriers, limiting the ability to adapt or refine business rules efficiently \cite{ori2024misalignment}.

The construction equipment industry provides a relevant example of these challenges. Market conditions vary across countries, machine lines, and size classes, and market data is derived from multiple internal and external sources that often differ in structure and classification standards \cite{cece2025,mordor2024construction}. In this context, accurate market share estimation requires integrating heterogeneous datasets and applying a series of transformations and mappings to construct a consistent foundation for analysis and reporting \cite{moller2024industrial}.

These challenges are directly reflected in the practice of Volvo Constr-uction Equipment (Volvo CE), a global provider of construction solutions with operations in more than 140 countries. At Volvo CE, market share estimation is implemented through an internal process known as Total Market Calculation (TMC), which operates within an SAP Business Warehouse (BW) environment and integrates heterogeneous datasets across regions, product categories, and machine segments.

However, due to the complexity of the underlying business logic and its system-level implementation in SAP, the TMC process lacks transparency for business users. It is difficult to trace how input data is transformed into final outputs, to verify whether the implemented logic aligns with business expectations, and to efficiently identify errors or inconsistencies. This results in reduced trust in the system and increased challenges in collaboration between business and IT stakeholders.

To address these limitations, this thesis adopts a Design Science Research approach and proposes the design and implementation of a data processing and visualization system. The proposed solution reconstructs the existing calculation logic as a structured and transparent data pipeline, enabling users to trace data transformations, validate intermediate results, and interact with the system more effectively. By bridging the gap between business understanding and technical implementation, the system supports improved transparency, more efficient error detection, and enhanced data-driven decision-making.

This thesis focuses on the early stages of the TMC workflow, particularly the preparation stage, in which heterogeneous data sources are integrated and cleaned, the adjustment stage, in which source-level values are reorganized and aligned, and the machine line split stage, in which aggregated values are redistributed into more granular categories. Together, these stages form the analytical foundation upon which subsequent market share calculations depend.





\section{Problem and Motivation}
The current TMC workflow at Volvo CE is operated within an SAP BW-based environment, where market-related data is loaded, mapped, calculated, and transferred into reporting through a combination of SAP objects, process chains, planning sequences, workbooks, and mapping tables. While this setup supports the required calculations, the business logic is distributed across multiple system-specific structures that are difficult for non-technical users to inspect or modify directly \cite{wong2016sap}. As a result, the overall calculation flow is not easily visible from a business perspective, and intermediate transformations cannot be followed step by step without specialized technical knowledge.

This limited transparency creates concrete problems in practice. When market share figures or intermediate values do not match expectations, tracing the source of a discrepancy is time-consuming, as users must navigate across multiple SAP objects to identify where in the process an issue was introduced. Furthermore, because the business logic is embedded in SAP-specific execution structures, the Volvo CE Strategy and Business Intelligence team is dependent on the support of dedicated Volvo IT personnel to execute the TMC workflow each year. In practice, this requires the involvement of one to two IT specialists per yearly cycle, with repeated back-and-forth communication between the business and IT sides to coordinate execution, handle data issues, and apply any necessary adjustments. This dependency increases both the time and coordination effort required to complete the yearly TMC cycle, and limits the ability of the business team to run, validate, or adjust the process independently.

These difficulties suggest that, although the existing SAP-based implementation supports the required calculations, it does not fully meet the business team's need for transparency, traceability, and independent validation. Therefore, the motivation for this thesis comes from the need for a more transparent and inspectable way to execute and review the early stages of the TMC process. More specifically, there is a clear interest in developing a platform that re-expresses the relevant workflow logic in a more accessible form, making it possible to follow calculations step by step, inspect intermediate outputs, and reduce operational dependence on IT. This thesis addresses this need by designing and implementing a web-based data processing and visualization system that reconstructs selected parts of the TMC workflow in a more transparent and user-oriented environment.

\section{Research Objective and Research Question}

The current TMC process integrates multiple data sources — TMA (Total Market reference data), SAL (Volvo CE internal sales data), OTH (supplementary external market data), and matrix-based business rules — within an SAP-driven environment whose logic is difficult to trace and interpret from a business user perspective.

The goal of this thesis is to design and implement an interactive data processing and visualization prototype for the Volvo CE TMC workflow that improves transparency, usability, and traceability in the preparation stage, including market-view derivation, as well as the adjustment and machine line split stages, by translating the underlying business logic into a web-based system that supports data upload, staged processing, and interactive result inspection.


The main research question is:
How can an interactive data processing and visualization prototype be designed and evaluated to enhance transparency, traceability, and usability in the Volvo CE TMC workflow?
This is addressed through three sub-questions:

\textbf{Sub-RQ1:} How can the architecture and data handling of a web-based prototype be designed to support structured upload, storage, and management of heterogeneous TMC workflow inputs, including source datasets and matrix-based business rules?\\

\textbf{Sub-RQ2:} How can the preparation, adjustment, and machine line split stages of the TMC workflow be re-expressed in a web-based environment so that intermediate transformations and derived results become inspectable and traceable for business users?\\

\textbf{Sub-RQ3:} To what extent does the implemented prototype improve transparency, usability, and traceability in the TMC workflow compared to the existing SAP-based representation, as assessed through demonstration and stakeholder feedback?\\

The expected outcome is a working web-based prototype system that re-expresses selected parts of the TMC workflow in a more accessible and inspectable environment, while also providing a foundation for future broader internal deployment and extension to later workflow stages.

\section{Scope and Delimitation}

This thesis focuses on the design and implementation of a web-based prototype for the Volvo CE TMC workflow, based on the existing SAP-driven calculation process that integrates multiple data sources, business rules, and matrix-based mappings.

The implemented scope covers source data upload, matrix input handling, preparation, market-view derivation, adjustment, and selected split/resplit-related processing. These stages include source data integration, rule-based mapping, intermediate data transformation, adjustment logic, and inspectable redistribution behaviour for selected split and resplit cases. The prototype provides interfaces for workflow navigation, layer-based visualization, interactive table exploration, filtering, summary calculation, and CSV export, so that users can review both stage-level outputs and selected derived results.

The thesis does not aim to replace the existing SAP system or implement the full TMC workflow. Full Total Market Calculation, full Restatement, and final Reporting are treated as future development rather than part of the implemented scope. The market-view output is used as an intermediate reference and inspection layer, not as a full replacement for the final TMC calculation and reporting stages.

This thesis should therefore be understood as an initial but functional prototype that demonstrates how selected TMC workflow logic can be translated into a more transparent, extensible, and user-friendly web-based system for business-oriented inspection and validation.








\section{Thesis Structure}

Following this introduction, the thesis is structured into six further chapters. The background chapter introduces the SAP BW environment, the TMC workflow, and key concepts related to data processing, mapping rules, and traceability. The methodology chapter describes the Design Science Research (DSR) approach used in the study. The problem and requirements chapter defines the practical problem and presents the requirements for the prototype. The design and implementation chapter explains the architecture, workflow logic, and implementation of the web-based artefact. The demonstration and evaluation chapter presents the execution of the prototype with 2024 workflow data and evaluates it against the defined requirements. Finally, the conclusions and discussion chapter answers the research questions, discusses contributions and limitations, and suggests future work.
