\section{Research Strategy}

Design Science Research (DSR) was selected as the research strategy for this thesis because the study aims to design and evaluate an artefact that addresses a practical problem in a real organisational context \cite{johannesson2021}. In this thesis, the artefact is a web-based prototype that reconstructs and visualises selected early stages of the Volvo CE Total Market Calculation (TMC) workflow outside the original SAP environment, including source data upload, preparation, adjustment, and machine line split.

DSR is suitable because the main contribution of the thesis is not only an analysis of the existing SAP-based workflow, but also the development of a prototype intended to improve transparency, usability, and traceability. The prototype re-expresses selected parts of the TMC process in a more inspectable form, making key transformations, data dependencies, business rules, and intermediate outputs easier to review.

To structure the research process, the thesis follows the five core DSR activities proposed by Johannesson and Perjons \cite{johannesson2021}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Explicate the Problem}: identify and clarify the practical problem addressed by the research.
    \item \textbf{Define Requirements}: translate the identified problem into requirements for the artefact.
    \item \textbf{Design and Develop the Artefact}: create the artefact based on the defined requirements.
    \item \textbf{Demonstrate the Artefact}: show how the artefact can be applied in a relevant context.
    \item \textbf{Evaluate the Artefact}: assess whether the artefact fulfils its intended purpose and addresses the identified problem.
\end{itemize}

Alternative research strategies were also considered. A case study strategy could have supported an in-depth investigation of the existing SAP-based TMC workflow, since case study research is commonly used to study a contemporary phenomenon within its real-world context \cite{yin2018}. However, a case study alone was considered insufficient because the aim of this thesis is also to design, develop, and evaluate a new technical artefact.

Action research was also considered, as it supports close collaboration with practitioners and intervention in organisational practice \cite{susman1978}. However, this thesis does not aim to introduce changes into the production TMC process or manage an organisational change initiative. The prototype is instead developed and assessed in a test setting as a research artefact. Therefore, DSR was considered more suitable as the main research strategy.

\subsection{Data Collection Methods}

This thesis used three main data collection methods: \textbf{document analysis}, \textbf{stakeholder discussions}, and \textbf{prototype-based data collection}. These methods were selected because the study required both contextual understanding of the existing SAP-based TMC workflow and technical material for designing, demonstrating, and evaluating the prototype.

Document analysis was used to examine TMC- and SAP-related process materials, workflow descriptions, source data structures, and matrix files. This method was suitable because documents can provide contextual evidence about organisational practices and existing work processes \cite{bowen2009document}. Stakeholder discussions and weekly review meetings were used to clarify business rules, user needs, validation expectations, feedback on prototype outputs, and workflow steps that were not fully visible from documentation alone. These discussions were semi-structured in nature, which allowed focused but flexible knowledge elicitation \cite{kallio2016systematic}.

Prototype-based data collection was used during design, demonstration, and evaluation. During development, CSV inputs, mapping tables, matrix rules, and generated intermediate outputs were inspected to support the reconstruction of selected TMC workflow stages. During demonstration and evaluation, the prototype was applied to a realistic TMC scenario based on the 2024 market data and matrix files used by Volvo CE for the yearly TMC calculation. The generated outputs and stakeholder feedback were then used to assess whether the prototype made the workflow easier to inspect and understand. Using multiple sources of evidence is suitable in software-oriented research in organisational settings \cite{runeson2009guidelines}.

Alternative data collection methods such as \textbf{large-scale surveys} and \textbf{direct SAP backend log extraction} were considered. However, a large-scale survey was less suitable because the TMC workflow involves a small group of specialised users and required detailed process-level understanding. Direct SAP backend log extraction was also outside the thesis scope. Therefore, the prototype was developed and assessed outside the SAP environment using Volvo CE's 2024 market data and matrix files.

\subsection{Data Analysis Method}

The collected material was analysed through a combination of \textbf{qualitative analysis} and \textbf{technical analysis}. The analysis included \textbf{workflow decomposition}, \textbf{requirements analysis}, \textbf{rule interpretation}, \textbf{structured output inspection}, and \textbf{requirement-based evaluation}. This was suitable because the thesis needed to interpret stakeholder input and workflow documentation, while also examining data structures, matrix-based rules, workflow dependencies, and prototype outputs. In DSR, analysis supports both problem understanding and artefact development and evaluation \cite{johannesson2021,hevner2004design}.

During problem explication and requirements definition, Volvo CE's TMC internal workflow documentation, related process materials, and stakeholder input were analysed to identify issues related to transparency, traceability, usability, validation support, and prototype performance. These findings were translated into functional requirements, which describe the artefact's main capabilities, and performance requirements, which address execution time, stability, and reliability \cite{johannesson2021}.

During design and development, source files, mapping tables, matrix rules, and selected SAP-related workflow descriptions were examined to reformulate parts of the TMC workflow into explicit backend processing steps. The analysis focused on decomposing workflow logic, interpreting rules, and inspecting intermediate outputs.

For demonstration and evaluation, generated outputs were analysed through output inspection, comparison with expected workflow logic, and requirement-based assessment. Stakeholder feedback was reviewed qualitatively and descriptively to identify comments related to transparency, usability, and inspectability. This supported the assessment of whether the artefact fulfilled its intended purpose in the Volvo CE context \cite{hevner2004design}.

Alternative methods such as \textbf{statistical survey analysis} and \textbf{controlled experiments} were considered but were less suitable because the artefact was evaluated as a prototype with a small group of specialised stakeholders. Therefore, the analysis focused on workflow understanding, output inspection, stakeholder feedback, and requirement-based evaluation.

\subsection{Design and Develop the Artefact}

The third DSR activity was to design and develop the artefact based on the requirements defined in the previous activity. Since the main contribution of this thesis is a working prototype, this activity formed a central part of the research process. Following Johannesson and Perjons, the activity involved imagining possible designs, selecting an appropriate design direction, building the artefact, and reflecting on the design decisions \cite{johannesson2021}.

In this stage, the selected data collection methods were applied in a more technical way. CSV-based source files, matrix tables, mapping rules, SAP-related workflow descriptions, and generated intermediate outputs were inspected to understand how selected parts of the TMC workflow could be reconstructed outside the SAP environment. Stakeholder clarification was also used when business rules, machine line mappings, or data dependencies were unclear.

The collected material was analysed through workflow decomposition, rule interpretation, and iterative output inspection. This analysis supported the translation of selected SAP-based workflow logic into explicit Python- and SQL-based backend processing steps. Instead of attempting to replicate the SAP environment directly, the artefact was designed as a modular web-based prototype with separate components for data upload, backend processing, structured storage, and frontend visualisation.

The development followed an iterative prototyping approach. Early design ideas focused on making the workflow more inspectable through staged outputs, workflow navigation, filtering, summary values, and export functions. These ideas were gradually implemented and refined as the understanding of the TMC workflow improved. The implementation scope was limited to source data upload, preparation, market-view derivation, adjustment, and selected machine line split/resplit-related processing, which represented the selected parts of the TMC workflow implemented in the prototype.

A key challenge in this activity was that important workflow logic in the original SAP-based process is embedded in planning functions, planning sequences, matrix-based structures, and staged transformations. These elements do not map directly onto reusable program code. They therefore had to be interpreted, decomposed, and reformulated into backend processing logic. The detailed system architecture, implementation logic, and interface design are presented in the artefact design and implementation chapters.

\section{Ethical Considerations}

This study was conducted in collaboration with Volvo CE and required careful attention to research integrity, confidentiality, and responsible handling of research material. The study was guided by principles of good research practice, including transparency, honesty, and respect in the conduct and reporting of research \cite{su_ethics_policy}.

The empirical part of the thesis involved workflow analysis, document review, prototype development, and informal discussions with relevant stakeholders. No sensitive personal data was collected or processed, and the study did not involve any physical intervention or any attempt to influence participants physically or mentally. Stakeholders who contributed contextual information were informed about the purpose of the study, and participation in discussions was voluntary \cite{etikprovningsmyndigheten_act}.

The purpose of these interactions was solely to understand current work practices and support the design and assessment of the artefact, not to evaluate or report individual performance or behavior. In this way, the study sought to respect participants’ autonomy while limiting the use of collected material to what was necessary for the research purpose \cite{vr_good_research_2024}.

Special consideration was also given to the handling of company-internal information. The prototype was developed and demonstrated in a controlled setting and was not used as a production replacement. Where screenshots, figures, code fragments, system outputs, or data samples from internal systems were included for explanatory purposes, they were anonymized or masked to remove company-specific identifiers and potentially sensitive information. The thesis does not disclose Volvo CE trade secrets, confidential business data, or complete proprietary calculation logic, and the handling of company-related material followed Volvo CE's internal requirements for confidentiality and responsible information handling \cite{su_ethics_policy}.

Overall, the study was conducted in accordance with Stockholm University’s ethical framework, Swedish principles for good research practice, and Volvo CE's internal requirements for confidentiality and responsible information handling, with particular attention to confidentiality, responsible data handling, and respect for the individuals involved in the research context \cite{vr_good_research_2024}.
